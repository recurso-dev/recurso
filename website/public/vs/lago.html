The license question

MIT vs AGPLv3 — why it matters

For many teams this is the deciding factor before a single feature gets compared.

Lago is AGPLv3 — a network copyleft license. If you modify Lago and users interact with your modified version over a network, you are obligated to offer them the modified source code. To be fair: running an unmodified Lago as a separate service alongside your product is generally workable. The friction starts when you want to fork it, patch it, or embed billing deep inside a proprietary system.

In practice, the bigger issue is often approval, not obligation. Many corporate legal teams restrict or outright prohibit AGPL software regardless of how it would be used — Google's open-source policy famously bans AGPL code internally. If you sell to or build inside such an organization, an AGPL billing core can be a non-starter before the engineering evaluation even begins.

Recurso is MIT. Fork it, patch it, embed it in a closed-source product, white-label it, ship it to customers — the only obligation is keeping the copyright notice. There is nothing for a legal review to escalate.

Architecture

What you'll actually run

Recurso: Go + PostgreSQL, ledger-first

A single Go binary against PostgreSQL. Every invoice and payment posts to a built-in double-entry ledger, with an optional TigerBeetle mirror for high-throughput accounting — PostgreSQL always remains the source of truth, so TigerBeetle is never a hard dependency.

On our benchmark hardware, Recurso sustains roughly 7.8k authenticated reads/s and about 34.6k invoices/min with full ledger posting (methodology and scripts are in the repo — run them on your own hardware).

Lago: Ruby, metering-first

Lago is built in Ruby and is strongest where billing is driven by high-volume usage events. If metering millions of events into rated charges is the core of your problem, that focus shows — and it's the honest reason to shortlist Lago.

If your team is Ruby-native, Lago's codebase will also feel more familiar to extend than a Go service, license permitting.

Where Recurso is honestly behind

Recurso is new: the community is small and there is no hosted cloud yet (the waitlist is open). EU VAT uses a static rate table rather than live rates, US sales tax runs through TaxJar behind an API key, and tax nexus is not modeled. Lago is well-funded, has a larger community, and you can pay for a managed product today.

The honest verdict

Which one should you run?

Choose Lago if…

  • High-volume usage metering is the heart of your billing problem
  • You want a hosted cloud you can buy today, from a well-funded vendor
  • A larger community and more production mileage matter to you
  • Your legal team has cleared AGPLv3, or you'll run it strictly unmodified

Choose Recurso if…

  • You need a permissive MIT license — to fork, embed, or ship inside proprietary software
  • You bill Indian customers: GST e-invoicing, RBI e-mandates, and DPDP consent are built in
  • You want a double-entry ledger under every invoice, not billing records alone
  • Failed-payment recovery matters: bandit-based dunning with recovered-revenue attribution
  • You want a small ops footprint: one Go binary plus PostgreSQL
