Recurso vs Flexprice

The license question

MIT vs AGPLv3 — why it matters

The same licensing calculus that applies to Lago applies here.

Flexprice is AGPLv3, a network copyleft license. Modify it and expose the modified version to users over a network, and you owe them the source. Running it unmodified as a standalone service is usually fine; forking, patching, or embedding it inside a proprietary product is where the obligation bites.

And independent of the strict obligation, plenty of corporate legal teams block AGPL software outright. If billing needs to live inside your closed-source product — or you sell into companies with AGPL bans — that constraint can end the evaluation early.

Recurso is MIT. Fork it, embed it, white-label it, ship it in a proprietary product — just keep the copyright notice. No copyleft, nothing for a license review to flag.

Architecture

Focused tool vs full engine

Recurso: broad and ledger-first

One Go binary against PostgreSQL, with every invoice and payment posted to a double-entry ledger and an optional TigerBeetle mirror (PostgreSQL stays authoritative). Subscriptions, invoicing, entitlements, dunning, FX-normalized MRR, webhook delivery tracking, and India compliance all live in one engine.

On our benchmark hardware: ~7.8k authenticated reads/s and ~34.6k invoices/min with full ledger posting. The scripts ship in the repo — reproduce them yourself.

Flexprice: usage + feature billing for AI

Flexprice is purpose-built for the AI/SaaS wave — metering token usage, API calls, and feature entitlements into charges. If that specific shape is your whole problem, a tool designed around it may fit more cleanly than a general engine, and that's a fair reason to try it.

As a younger project it moves quickly, which cuts both ways: fresh ideas, but less production mileage and a smaller community than more established tools.

Where Recurso is honestly behind

Recurso is new with a small community and no hosted cloud yet. EU VAT is a static rate table, US sales tax runs through TaxJar behind a key, and tax nexus isn't modeled. If your needs are narrowly AI-usage-metering, a specialist tool may be a tighter fit than Recurso's breadth.

The honest verdict

Which one should you run?

Choose Flexprice if…

  • Your product is AI/usage-native and metering + feature billing is the core problem
  • You want a tool designed from scratch around modern usage-based pricing
  • You don't need accounting-grade ledgering or India compliance
  • Your legal team has cleared AGPLv3, or you'll run it unmodified

Choose Recurso if…

  • You want a permissive MIT license you can embed anywhere
  • You need one engine for subscriptions, invoicing, entitlements, and dunning
  • You bill Indian customers and need GST, RBI e-mandates, and DPDP consent built in
  • You want a double-entry ledger and FX-normalized MRR under your numbers
  • You value a small ops footprint: one Go binary plus PostgreSQL
